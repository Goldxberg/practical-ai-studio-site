Process

Fast enough to move, structured enough to keep.

I keep the process tight so the work does not disappear into “AI exploration.” The point is to leave with a working system, not a stack of ideas.

Step 1

Map the workflow.

We identify the bottleneck, the data source, the human decision points, and what should remain manual.

Step 2

Build the smallest useful version.

One workflow, one entry point, one reliable output. Enough to prove value without building a machine you cannot maintain.

Step 3

Refine and hand off.

Once the core works, I tighten prompts, logic, UI, and process notes so the system feels usable instead of fragile.

What I avoid: long strategy decks, stacked subscriptions you do not need, and AI demos that look impressive but create more maintenance than value.

What I optimize for: leverage, clarity, speed, and systems your team actually wants to keep using next month.

Why this works

The value is not “AI.” The value is better operations.

The strongest projects usually change one of these three things: how information moves, how decisions get made, or how much manual effort is needed to keep the system running.

Less manual coordination

Fewer steps bouncing between inboxes, spreadsheets, notes, and chat threads just to get a single task finished.

Cleaner team context

Important information becomes easier to retrieve, easier to summarize, and easier to act on without asking the same questions twice.

Stronger product surfaces

When AI belongs in the product, it should feel native to the workflow instead of pasted on top as a novelty feature.

Good AI work feels almost boring after launch. The workflow is cleaner, the team is faster, and nobody has to think about the machinery every five minutes.
